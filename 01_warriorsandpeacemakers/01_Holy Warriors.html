    The concept of religious warriors was well established in the Middle Ages, but the number of holy warriors and the and scale of the battles they fought in the early modern period was unprecedented. Whether in Europe, the Americas, or Asia, soldiers and commanders saw themselves as religious actors carrying out the will of God, and freely used religion to justify their bellicose activities.
    A typical example of such a holy warrior was John Underhill, a commander of the English forces during the Pequot War of 1634-38, which devastated the Pequot tribe. The root cause of the war was the English conviction that tribes like the Pequots were agents in a diabolical conspiracy against God’s chosen people, which provided a religious justification for taking up arms against the Indians. The bloody climax of the conflict came with the burning of a Pequot fort in Mystic, Connecticut, strikingly portrayed in this fold-out map from Nevves from America, Underhill’s account of the war. After an unsuccessful attempt to take the fort, the English forces set it ablaze, burning most of the Pequots alive, and leaving the rest to be cut down by English rifles or arrows fired by their Narraganset allies. Overall, between 400 and 700 Pequots were estimated to have died in the attack.
    The massacre proved shocking to Indians and English alike. Even Underhill realized the inhumane nature of it all, surmising that his readers might justifiably ask, “…should not Christians have more mercy and compassion?” Even so, he, like many other holy warriors of his time, justified his actions in religious terms, pointing to the Old Testament accounts of the wars of King David to prove that “We had sufficient light from the word of God for our proceedings.”
