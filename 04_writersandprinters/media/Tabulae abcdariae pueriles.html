    1. 1 2017-06-21T14:36:06+00:00 Printing Religious Jobs 7 gallery 2017-08-14T19:19:20+00:00
      Religion had been at the center of the printing industry from the 1450s.  Indeed, the Roman church was the first major customer for the early printers. By the time of the Reformation, this this relationship had become so close that a church’s survival could depend on its ability to use print to spread its view of “true” Christianity. Printed religious products included not only extensive works like Bibles, treatises, and catechisms, but also more ephemeral works that were essential to bringing Christianity to the public.
      Before Johannes Gutenberg printed his great Bible, the first works to come off his press were more mundane publications: indulgences, schoolbooks, broadsheets, and small pamphlets. Gutenberg needed these small jobs to finance larger projects, and the church needed them to support its work of instructing and engaging with the laity. Throughout the early modern period, printers continued to receive work orders for small, ephemeral publications that met a specific – often very basic – need quickly. The Alphabet Table for Children is an outstanding example of this sort of job: the broadside displayed the letters of the alphabet and the Lord’s Prayer, and would have been posted in schoolrooms for students to recite from and memorize. In this way, printers helped churches ensure that students learned religion alongside basic literacy.
      Naturally, religious authorities recognized the religious value of print, and they often did whatever possible to support the printers in their work, sometimes by providing technical assistance to printers so they could produce an ever more diverse array of religious texts. Various Alphabets is a testament to this desire; the book includes print specimens of various unfamiliar and obscure languages (including the alleged language of Adam, seen here), many of which were taken from specific manuscripts or inscriptions. The Congregation for the Propagation of the Faith produced and distributed these specimens so that printers could make the typefaces necessary to print these languages, which in turn would contribute to the Congregation’s mission of improving Biblical scholarship and bringing the faith to new audiences in the Near East.
