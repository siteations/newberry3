    1. 1 media/ayer_888_j91_1691_tp_o3_cropped.jpg 2017-06-21T14:35:13+00:00 Religious Poetry: New Issues, New Voices 16 image_header 2017-09-08T19:48:14+00:00 Detail from Poemas de la única poetisa americana, musa dezima by Sor Juana Inés de la Cruz. Bareclona: Joseph Llopis, 1691.
      Poetry had always offered an outlet for religious thinkers to talk about Christianity in ways that prose treatises or commentaries did not easily accommodate. The increasing complexity of early modern faith made it possible and necessary for poets to represent more aspects of the complicated Christian experience. We can see this plainly in the three extraordinary women gathered here, all of whom were celebrated as poets by their contemporaries.
      Some poets reflected the fluid theological atmosphere of the early 16th century, before categories like “Protestant” and “Catholic” had been clearly defined. At this time, individuals could make their own formulation of Christianity by picking and choosing among competing religious ideas. In Italy, for example, a collection of clergy, artists, intellectuals, and nobles known as the spirituali worked to reform the Roman Church. Among them was the noblewoman Vittoria Colonna, whose Spiritual Verses, inspired by Michelangelo, espoused a passionate and interiorized theology that was centered on Christ.
      Early modern poetry also reflected the pain and uncertainty that arose from religious conflict, as writers looked for ways to come to terms with their religious choices. A century after Colonna, this was certainly true of Anne Bradstreet, whose The Tenth Muse Lately Sprung up in America revealed a devout Puritan’s attempt to make sense of her religion. Her poetry was rooted in the mission to live a godly life, and included reflections on the tensions between Christian patience and suffering the evils of a fallen world. Though she openly gave voice to the doubt and despair that could arise from this tension, she remained resolute. In “A Dialogue between Old England and New,” for instance, she depicted her native land seeking forgiveness for unjustly driving out the pious Puritans in favor of “stinking, stigmatizing, Romish clerks.”
      Devotional religious poetry flourished in colonial Latin America as well. Sor Juana Inés de la Cruz, a leading author of the Spanish Golden Age, was known in her time as the Tenth Muse. Born in Mexico, she knew a church whose people were a patchwork of Spaniards, creoles (native-born Mexicans of Spanish heritage), black slaves, Indians, and those of mixed race; all of these non-European Christians were given a voice in her villancicos, the popular carol sequences performed at festivals. She also addressed religious matters that deeply mattered to these groups, especially creoles like herself; this sonnet from The Poems of the Only Poetess in America, for instance, celebrates the cult of the Virgin of Guadalupe, who stood at the center of creole spirituality and, in later years, politics.
