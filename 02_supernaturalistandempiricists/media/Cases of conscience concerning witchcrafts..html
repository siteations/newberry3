    1. 1 media/case_ms_b_8884_558_55_o2_cropped.jpg 2017-06-20T14:43:00+00:00 Witchcraft and the Rise of the Empirical 9 image_header 2017-08-15T21:45:11+00:00 Detail from Cases of conscience concerning witchcrafts by Increase Mather. 1700.
      The most notorious example of the role of the supernatural in the early modern period was the worldwide persecution of witches, which increased dramatically in the later 16th century. This unprecedented wave of violence – thousands of people, most of them lower-class women, suffered torture and death – demonstrated the important place of the supernatural in early modern culture, and sowed the seeds for its decline.

      During the 15th century, the Roman church wanted more control over the practice of lay piety, and made a more concerted effort to convince the laity that all forms of magical activity outside of the church were demonic. Inquisitors, preachers, and theorists worked diligently to spread this message and root out potential witches. Many of them did so with the Hammer of Witches in hand. The most expansive and influential medieval manual for witch hunters, the book provided proof for the existence and threat of witches, offered remedies for their victims, and set guidelines for detecting and prosecuting them.

      Amidst the religious upheaval of the Reformation, Christians of all sects were more inclined to see diabolical forces at work undermining the religious, moral, and social order. This made witchcraft a problem for the secular authorities, who were willing to use the legal system to end this spiritual threat. The growing number of juridical proceedings against witches called for a more effective textbook than the Hammer, which many believed depended too heavily on folklore and superstition. A replacement came in the form of Martin Antoine Del Rio’s Six Books of Inquiries into Magical Things, a careful synthesis of witchcraft that drew connections with mathematics, astrology, alchemy, and other disciplines, and argued that the surge in witchcraft was rooted in the spread of “heresy” during the Reformation.

      A more empirical approach figured in the decline of the witch hunts near the end of the 17th century. Many religious authorities argued that the devil had no power to impact reality – that belonged to God alone – so witches posed no threat. Many were also concerned about the miscarriages of justice that often occurred during witch persecutions. Both these concerns are clear in Increase Mather’s Cases of Conscience Concerning Witchcraft, a report on the Salem Witch Trials. Although Mather defended the actions of the court that executed 20 alleged witches, he also expressed doubts about some of the supernatural evidence used during the trials. In the opening here, for instance, Mather doubted that “spectral evidence” (information from diabolical sources), was viable. Such information was enough “…to cause an enquiry into the truth of things,” he wrote, but it could not be used as “…evidence or grounds of conviction.”
