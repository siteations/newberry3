    1. 1 2017-06-21T14:36:37+00:00 The Business of Religious Printing 6 gallery 2017-08-14T19:25:36+00:00
      However much it was intertwined with religion in the early modern period, the fact remained that printing was a big business. Overall, religious materials dominated production, but not all religious materials were equally popular, forcing printers to strike a balance between serving the church and securing their business. The items here give some sense of how early modern printers navigated this difficult challenge.
      Printers were not immune to the religious fervor of their day, of course.  Many held strong religious convictions that powerfully shaped their output. In some cases, printers were chary about revealing their religious allegiances. A counter-example is offered by the English printer and bookseller Henry Hills, who left no doubt concerning his Catholic affiliation. A Catalogue of Works Printed by Henry Hills was a fairly standard list of the titles he sold and their prices, but readers could hardly have missed the first line, which clearly told them “no Protestants” would be found in his shop.
      Successful printers had learned to measure the pulse of their reading public and were always eager to print what their customers wanted. They had no qualms about providing the public with more offbeat religiously-themed products. The Playing Cards shown here, for instance, provide a scathing Protestant satire on the pro-Catholic reign of James II that also celebrates the Glorious Revolution of 1688, but in a very popular format. The religious intent of this pack is unmistakable - the card shown here depicts English Protestants openly resisting a Jesuit denouncing the English translation of the Bible – but the setting in which it would have been experienced was much more relaxed and jovial than that of most religious debates.
